# How Can We Improve Win Rates?

## Introduction

Most people would guess that athletes improve by competing - but by how much and does the type of competition matter? 
The charts in this post show how athletes can improve performance by competing more, competing more frequently, and exploring different types of competition.

The first set of charts shows how match outcomes change as athletes **compete in more events**.
The next explores the impact of **entering the absolute division**, and how overall win rates and submissions are affected.

Next we look at overall match performance based on variety of **gi versus no-gi** events, showing how competing in a variety of styles can influence success.

Recent activity matters too: athletes who have more matches in the **past six months** generally see faster improvements, especially in submission wins.

Finally, **the number of teammates** at an event subtly affects submission outcomes, highlighting how team composition can impact results even when overall win rates stay similar.

**NOTE:** All analysis is based on publicly available historical match data. 
A small number of matches are excluded due to incomplete results or limited visibility, but the dataset covers the vast majority of recorded competitions. 
Match and event data are collected for events in the USA only and for years 2017-2025.

## Improvement 1: Compete More Often

The charts below show how match outcomes change as athletes gain more competition experience.
The x-axis groups athletes by which event they are competing in (first event, second event, third or fourth event, etc.).

The left panel shows outcome rates, while the right panel shows how many matches fall into each experience group.

![Monthly Volume]({{ 'assets/images/2026-03-05/fig01-stats_by_event_index.png' | relative_url }})

### Win Rate Steadily Improves With Experience 
Athletes in their first event win about **45% of matches**, but 
start winning more often than losing by their **third or fourth competition**.  athletes competing in their **21st event or later win about 68%**.

### Submissions Become More Common as Athletes Gain Experience
In first events, only about **20% of matches are won by submission**. It takes around **15 to 20 competitions** for that rate to double.
On average, most competitors start winning by submission more than losing by submission after 3 or 4 events.

### Experience Drops Sharply
Most competitors only compete a few times.
The number of matches falls dramatically after the first event, and continues declining with each additional
stage of competition experience.

## Improvement 2: Compete in Absolute

This chart shows what effect competing in the absolute division has on win and loss rates.

The **x-axis shows how many times an athlete has entered the absolute division**, ranging from those who have never competed, 
to athletes who have entered once, 2–3 times, or more than 3 competitions in absolute.  

The figure has two panels:

- The left panel shows match outcome rates: overall wins, wins by submission, and losses by submission.  
- The right panel shows the number of athletes grouped by number of absolute bracket entries. 

![Absolute]({{ 'assets/images/2026-03-05/fig02-stats_by_absolute.png' | relative_url }})


### Most Athletes Avoid Absolute

Approximately **91%** of athletes never competed in the absolute division. Of the **9%** who have competed, about **70%** compete exactly once. 
continue to compete in absolute beyond their first event.

### Win Rate Increases 
Going from zero competitions in absolute to beyond 4 competitions, athletes win rate increases from **48.8%** to **59.2%**.  This
could show competing in absolute improves win rates, or it shows more successful athletes tend to gravitate towards competing in absolute.

### You Submit More Often Than Get Submitted
After competing in an Absolute division just once, you are more likely to win by submission than lose by submission.  That gap widens with
every additional competition entered.

## Improvement 3: Compete in Gi and No-Gi (But mostly No-Gi)

In the charts below, athletes are grouped by the number of times they entered a bracket by style, not by how many matches they had in each style.

Every event an athlete enters counts as a gi event and/or a no-gi based on if they competed in at least one match of that style regardless of bracket size.  
These are totaled for every athlete and grouped by what percentage of events were in gi vs no-gi.

* **Gi Specialist** – Competed in **only gi events** (100% gi).
* **Mostly Gi** – Competed in mostly gi events, with some no-gi (60–99% gi).
* **Balanced** – Competed in a fairly even mix of gi and no-gi events (40–59% gi).
* **Mostly No Gi** – Competed mostly in no-gi events, with some gi (1–39% gi).
* **No Gi Specialist** – Competed in **only no-gi events** (0% gi).

![stats by style]({{ 'assets/images/2026-03-05/fig03-stats_by_style.png' | relative_url }})

### Most People Are Specialists

Although balanced is the most common group with **36.9%** of athletes, **50.8%** of athletes compete exclusively in either gi or no-gi with **31.8%** being exclusive to gi.  
Fun fact: gi specialists are the only group more likely to lose than win and more likely to get submitted than to submit an opponent. 
This suggest that those who compete exclusively in the gi are losers, statistically speaking of course!

### Balanced is Good, But Lean Towards No-Gi
Aside from no-gi specialists, the more no-gi events an athlete enters, the better their stats become.  Both specialist categories are popular, and both are
the least successful as well. Athletes in either specialist categories would improve in all metrics by competing at least once in the other style.

## Improvement 4: Compete Frequently

The chart below shows how win rates are affected by the number of matches the athlete had competed in, in the previous 6 months at the time of the event. 
The bottom chart shows how many matches fall in each grouping. 
For example, the two largest bars show that most athletes about to compete have between 2 and 7 matches in the past 6 months.

![Skill Distribution]({{ 'assets/images/2026-03-05/fig04-stats_by_period_180.png' | relative_url }})

### High Competition Frequency Improves Faster Than A Longer Career
All win rates improve with more matches, but these improvements are steeper than from section 1 based on number of events over the entire career.  The biggest change
happens to submission wins with a **42%** relative increase from first match in 6 months to the 9th-16th match, going from  **21.4%** to **30.4%**

### The First Match Goes Better than the Second
Competitors entering an event with **one recent match** actually perform slightly worse than those entering with none. Because most athletes compete more than once per event,
this usually means the second group is the second match of the day.  Win rate drops by about **2.2% relative to the first match**, 
falling from **46.5%** to **45.5%**.  Submission losses also move in the wrong direction. 
The rate of losing by submission **increases by about 6.1%**, rising from **26.4% to 28.0%** for the second match of the day.

After this point, performance begins improving steadily as recent match activity increases.

For the first competition back, don't burn yourself out on the first match. ** *Pacing is important!* **

### Some People Compete Like It's Their Job
There are just over 4,000 matches that have occurred where an athlete has more than 64 matches in the previous 6 months!  They win over **70%** of the time and 
win by submission **54%** of their matches.  They are a very small subset, but they are very good at what they do!

## Improvement 5: Beware the Lone Wolf
Interestingly enough, the number of teammates who compete the same at the same event as you has almost no impact on win rates, but it does impact submission rates!

The chart below shows the difference between:

- the percentage of wins that happen by submission
- the percentage of losses that happen by submission

Positive values mean athletes are **more likely to win by submission than lose by submission**.

![teammates]({{ 'assets/images/2026-03-05/fig05-sub_win_gap_by_teammates.png' | relative_url }})

### Athletes With More Teammates Get Submitted More Than They Submit Others
Once the team size at an event is 4 or larger, the odds of an athlete losing by submission are higher than by winning. 

This likely reflects variation in skill within larger teams. Bigger teams tend to include athletes across many belt levels, 
meaning some competitors may be more vulnerable to submissions even if the overall win rate remains stable.


### Clubs with 1 or 2 Members Are Submission Hunters
The curve is the steepest in the beginning, starting at **5%** more likely to win by submission than lose. This drops to **3%** with a team size of 2.  

Interpretation is less clear after the number of team members grows beyond 2 because club results are averaged together.  
My interpretation shows clubs tend to have 1-2 really strong submission hunters and they have no problems competing alone.


## Summary

Athletes who compete more often—both over their career and in recent months—tend to win more matches and earn more submission victories. Consistent activity pays off.
Entering the absolute division or competing in a mix of gi and no-gi events also helps. Diversifying competition experience improves overall performance.
Team size at an event subtly affects submissions. Larger teams may include athletes of varying skill levels, which can influence outcomes even if overall wins stay stable.

In short, compete often, compete frequently, diversify how you compete, and don't be afraid to compete alone (and be a little suspicious of those that do).
